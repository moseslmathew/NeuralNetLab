Massive Data
An Ocean of Data The internet became the world's largest training set

Models are only as good as the data they learn from — and suddenly there was an ocean of it.

Billions of text documents, images, and videos became available for training. Curated datasets like Common Crawl, Wikipedia dumps, and ImageNet gave researchers the raw material to build ever more powerful models.

Instead of manually programming rules, we fed the machines human history, and they learned the patterns.

GPU Power
From Gaming to AI NVIDIA's CUDA platform (2007) turned gaming graphics cards into parallel computing monsters.

Neural networks require an astronomical amount of simple matrix multiplication. A modern CPU has 8 to 24 powerful cores built for complex, multi-step logic, making them too slow for AI because they handle these tasks sequentially.

A modern AI GPU, however, packs thousands of smaller cores (often 10,000+). While not built for complex logic, they calculate thousands of simple operations simultaneously. This massive parallel architecture is perfect for AI.

1000x faster: By 2012, AlexNet trained on two gaming GPUs crushed the ImageNet competition, proving hardware was no longer the bottleneck.

Transformers
"Attention Is All You Need" 2017 — The Google Research paper that started it all

This single algorithm is the backbone of GPT, BERT, and virtually every large language model you've heard of.

Before Transformers, models processed text sequentially, one word at a time, often forgetting the beginning of a sentence by the time they reached the end.

The self-attention mechanism changed everything. It allows the model to look at all parts of the input simultaneously, dynamically deciding which words are most relevant to each other, unlocking unprecedented logic, reasoning, and context understanding.

TRAINING

Learning Without Rules

Imagine building a robot to play football — without programming it with any rules on how to play. Instead, it learns entirely through training.

⚽
01
Takes Action
The robot moves, passes, or shoots — with no prior knowledge of what's right or wrong.
📡
02
Gets Feedback
Each action receives feedback — was that move good or bad? Did it lead closer to the goal?
🔗
03
Adjusts Connections
Good action? Strengthen those neural connections. Bad action? Weaken them. The network rewires itself.
🏆
04
Recognizes Patterns
Over time, the robot learns which actions lead to better outcomes — and performance improves.
No one taught it the rules. Through thousands of iterations of action → feedback → adjustment, the network discovered them on its own.
